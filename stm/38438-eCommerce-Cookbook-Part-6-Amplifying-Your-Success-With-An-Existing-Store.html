    eCommerce Cookbook Part 6 - Amplifying Your Success With An Existing Store

    So you've got some sales rolling in, and you're in profit.

    Job done, right?

    Definitely not.

    Just like in affiliate marketing, the hustle really starts after you see green.

    This is where you take your single successful product, and turn that into a sustainable asset that prints cash for you.

    The most successful stores are the ones which master the principle of constantly increasing their edge. It's not enough to just make sales - you should be finding ways to make sure that every sale makes you more and more money, and builds you a bigger and bigger edge over new competitors coming into your space.

    Here's a checklist of the things you need to be doing! This is a huge topic, so each one of these has literally had books written about it, so this post is designed to be a roadmap of starting points on your journey.

    There are 4 areas to consider:

    • Getting More Money From Each Sale
    • Care And Feeding Of Repeat Customers
    • Supply-side optimisation
    • Conversion Rate Optimisation



    1: Getting More Money From Each Sale

    Upsells! Down-sells! And cross-sells, and sells in every other kind of direction.

    Upsells have been called the most powerful tool in Internet commerce, and for a good reason.

    The central concept is very, very simple: when's the time that someone is most likely to buy from you? When they're actually staring at a checkout with their credit card out, ready to buy.

    So don't just settle for a single sale at that point - try to increase the value of the sale.

    That can be by suggesting a more deluxe version of the thing they're already buying - an upsell. Or it can be by offering different, complimentary products - a cross-sell.

    You'll have seen these techniques at work on Amazon. They work great there and they can work great for your store too. So start testing upsells as soon as you can - there are great Shopify apps out there to make that easier.

    You should also look into down-sells. This is where you convert a customer who has decided not to buy by offering a cheaper option. You've got to balance this carefully to not convert customers who would otherwise have bought a more expensive option, but if you get it right it's very powerful. This can also be one of the points where retargeting techniques can really come into play.

    Do this successfully and you build a huge advantage over any newcomers in your niche by making much bigger margins on each sale.

    Key search terms for further reading: Upsell, Cross-sell, down-sell and everything that accompanies each of those. Also, you should look into pricing psychology at this point.



    2: Care And Feeding Of Repeat Customers

    When you're growing a store, it's almost always easier to sell to someone who has bought from you before.

    So keep a really close eye on your repeat customer numbers: those are some of the most important numbers in your store. Here's a good basic article from Shopify on those metrics.

    And keep in touch with anyone who has bought from you once. Send them thank-you emails. Check in with them to see how their product suited them. And of course keep your email list warm and running with useful stuff - not just sales emails.

    And once you've done that, a significant amount of your product launch time should be devoted to selling to your repeat customers. What will they buy? What offers will persuade them to come back? And of course, what do they want?

    When I'm consulting on ecommerce stores, one of the most common mistakes I've seen is lack of focus on repeat customers. All things being equal, unless you've really tapped out this resource already or your store sells true one-time-only products (very rare), you'll make more money trying to sell existing customers more products rather than trying to find new customers to sell to.

    As a bonus, once you've got your existing customer funnel nailed, you should also start to look at nearly-customers: people who came to your site, showed some interest, but didn't buy anything... yet. Retarget them, get them on your list, and then start working on converting them to actual-customers.

    Your customer and nearly-customer base are a huge competitive advantage - essentially you've got access to a high-converting traffic source that new competitors won't have. So take full advantage of that.

    Key search terms for further reading: customer retention. Also anything and everything about running email lists.



    3: Supply-side optimisation

    Don't just focus on how much money you're making: you also need to focus on how much money you're spending.

    Once you're selling any kind of significant volume, it's time to get in touch with your suppliers and start negotiating your prices down. Given that you're buying on AliExpress, the chances are you're already paying a fairly major markup, so some negotiation can pay dividends.

    First tip: get off email and onto WeChat. You'll generally get faster response times and generally better communications with your suppliers there rather than email.

    Second tip: do your research. Check out other major markets to see if the product you're selling is offered cheaper there. Even if you're not able to buy off those markets as easily, knowing the cost elsewhere can help in negotiations.

    Third tip: open the conversation. Negotiation is, obviously, kind of a large topic (understatement!) but the most important element is just getting started. Don't ask, don't get.

    Fourth tip: don't just think money. Want the items branded with your store's logo and URL? Want better/faster shipping? Want to make some changes to the product design? Want a catalogue of your other products included with each package sent out? There are lots of things you can negotiate for beyond just lower prices.

    Having sales rolling in is a big competitive advantage if you leverage it properly, because you can get the kind of costs and efficiencies in your pipeline that people just testing out a new store won't be able to achieve.

    Key search terms for further reading: Negotiation. I'd strongly recommend picking up a copy of "Getting To Yes" on your Kindle. Also anything related to sourcing from Aliexpress.



    4: Conversion Rate Optimisation

    Now that you have some sales coming in, it's time to start looking for leaks in your sales funnel, and plugging the hell out of them.

    Conversion rate optimisation is a huge topic that many people spend their entire careers on, but the basics are simple: for each step in your funnel, figure out what could be stopping people from converting, and then test ways to overcome that stopping point.

    Key things to consider:

    • Split-testing. I've already written kind of a lot about this elsewhere on STM, so for now I'll just say that it's as important to split-test in ecommerce as any other area of internet marketing. Once you have enough traffic to make it useful, test images, test copy, test buttons, test headlines, and keep testing. Unfortunately split-testing in Shopify is kind of a pain in the ass, so your best bet is to sign up with one of the well-known split-test services out there. Visual Website Optimiser would probably be my choice, although there's no ideal option.
    • Speed. Once you're making sales, it's time to look at your page loading speeds. Pingdom's page speed tool is my usual choice for this, but do remember to check from the same geographical location as your users. If you're seeing load times of above a second from your geo, it's time to look into a faster-loading store. Check the apps you're using for loading speed - look at the waterfall graph to check if any of them are misbehaving - and consider investing in a fast-loading theme. I've heard good things about a new theme called Turbo lately. It's not cheap, but speed is worth paying for.
    • Customer Feedback. The biggest advantage an ecommerce store owner has over a classic affiliate play is that ecomm store owners actually get to talk to their customers. So take full advantage of that. Ask your customers for feedback, consider it, and test it. Is there an area of the store they found clunky? Is there something they wanted that wasn't there? Did they have a concern about the product that wasn't met? Likewise, talk to people who didn't buy from you too. Someone put a message on your Facebook ad saying "THIS LOOKS AWESOME!!!!oneoneone" but didn't buy? Message them and ask them -nicely - what happened and how you can improve! Remember: negative comments on your FB ads count as customer feedback!
    • Testimonials. You've got actual customers now - time to leverage that to maximum advantage. Social proof is incredibly powerful - so once you've asked customers how their product experience has been, if they're really happy, ask them if you can quote them on your store. Ideally get a picture of them up there. You'll see massive conversion rate hikes from good testimonials. (Now's also the time to get an automated app for asking for customer reviews going!)


    A well-optimised funnel is a killer advantage, and one that's very hard to replicate for new competitors.

    Key search terms for further reading: Look into "Conversion Rate Optimisation", and prepare to dive down a rabbit-hole. I like http://www.copyhackers.com as one good accessible starting point.

    And that's it for this part! In the next and final part of the eCommerce Cookbook, we'll look at the world beyond Facebook - how do you wean yourself off dependency on the big blue monster and find other ways to drive traffic to your store?

    A few quick comments:

    A/B testing only makes sense when you have enough traffic. So if you're doing 10 sales per day, just forget it and work on building up traffic.

    For getting reviews - make sure this is automated. Make sure you have an email sequence auto-trigger on purchase that asks to leave a review. There are apps for that. I use one as well to make my life easier and get reviews on auto pilot.

    Originally Posted by najamx
    Your Cookbook still cooking in 2019 ?
    The fundamentals remain the same.

    Although the whole Shopify+Aliexpress model isn't remotely as lucrative anymore, it's still a good starting point for newbies, and a great testing ground to uncover promising products.

    After you find some promising products, you can proceed to source them directly from manufacturers, and either package and send to customers yourself, or use a local fulfillment centre.

    At that point, you can also look into branding. Many manufacturers will print your logo on their goods for you at small cost (or for free when you satisfy minimum order requirements).

    There are actually still people that are making money from the dropshipping from Aliexpress model, although it's definitely not remotely as easy as it was a couple years back. You'll need to get more creative - choose products with better profit margins, that are not saturated, write good copy, come up with good ad angles etc. Implementing upsells/downsells/cross-sells to maximize front-end profits is a good thing to do. Lastly, building ONE site PER product can really boost conversion rates compared to just sending the customer to a product page on a Shopify store.

    Have fun!
