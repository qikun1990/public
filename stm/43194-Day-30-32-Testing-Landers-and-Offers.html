    Day 30-32: Testing Landers and Offers

    ***********************************

    READING

    Finally - we get to learn how to test and optimize our way to profits!

    To refresh your memory, I would like you to re-read parts of previous lessons at this time.

    Firstly, please re-read the EXPLANATION section of "First Lesson on Testing and Optimization":

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...ation&p=358579

    Quoting that lesson: In a nutshell, here's the testing and optimization strategy we'll be following in the next few lessons:


    1)Split-test landing pages and offers until finding a combination that turns enough traffic segments profitable, to result in a satisfactory amount of total profits across these green segments.

    2)At the traffic source, either a)target the profitable traffic segments (an approach also referred to as "whitelisting") or b)exclude the unprofitable traffic segments (an approach also referred to as "blacklisting"). (The testing of bids is also a necessary part of this process - more on this in a future lesson.)

    Basically, point 1) above focuses on TESTING, whereas point 2) focuses on OPTIMIZATION.

    Next, please re-read the "General Offer+Lander Testing Strategy" section in this lesson:

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...Lander-Testing

    Below, we'll learn how to execute that testing strategy in detail.



    ***********************************

    ACTION & EXPLANATION


    Bird's Eye View

    One of the biggest keys to reaching profits, is the need to FIRST focus on testing offers and landers to find an offer+lander combination that can make a big-enough portion of the total traffic profitable, and THEN proceed to tweak campaign targeting - such as cutting the unprofitable traffic.

    And what constitutes a "big-enough portion of the total traffic"? Basically something you can target at the traffic network - a group of profitable placements, a profitable OS, a profitable browser, etc. etc. - that can give you enough daily profits - which shouldn't be below (say) $5-10/day even for the newest affiliate. (Otherwise, you may as well go work at McDonald's!)

    Too many new affiliates try to skimp on the testing process - by taking a sub-par offer and/or lander and then brute-forcing their way to profits by focusing on heavy-cutting of placements etc.

    That is NOT the way to go. In most cases, they end up spending lots of money in the cutting process, only to be left with tiny profits from the bits of traffic left over from the heavy cutting.

    So, make this your mantra:


    Test Landers and Offers until the Profitable Portion of the Traffic has potential of yielding your Target Daily Profits and ROI, and then Tweak Campaign Targeting to Optimize Further.



    *****

    How Often to Check Stats

    So you've launched your campaign with one or more offers and multiple landers. Stats are starting to come in.

    You've verified tracking to be correct, and have started a campaign journal for this campaign.

    What you do next, is check your stats regularly and apply the instructions in the rest of this lesson whenever possible.

    "How regularly?" you may ask.

    The faster the traffic comes in, the more frequently you should be checking your stats. In the beginning, try to check once per hour if you can - you'll soon know whether you're checking too frequently or not, and adjust accordingly.

    If you're not able to check stats often, either:

    1)Throttle/distribute your traffic at the traffic source so that traffic will flow more slowly (e.g. set a small daily budget and throttle traffic to reach this spend every 24 hours), or

    2)Put the campaign on pause at the traffic source whenever you're too busy to check stats, and resume when you're free.

    The less-often you check stats, the more money you can potentially waste, as you "overrun" offers, landers, and placements past the point where they can be identified as being unpromising and paused at the traffic source.

    However, if you check stats too often, you'd be wasting valuable time you could be using to do more productive work (e.g. setting up new campaigns, scaling profitable campaigns). So you'll need to find a balance.

    As mentioned, every time you check stats, check for the conditions listed below, and if any of the conditions is true, take the prescribed actions.

    And for every major change you make to your campaign, don't forget to make a note in your Campaign Journal!



    *****

    Identify a Good-Enough Offer For Cutting Landers


    Condition to Check For: See whether any of the offers has made a total of 2 conversion or more, with a conversion rate of one conversion per 10x payout in spend or better.

    Action to Take: Pause all offers except the one that meets this condition.

    (Note: "10x payout in spend" means "10 times the offer payout in traffic cost". For example, if the offer payout is $1, and you've spent $10 or more on the offer without seeing a single conversion, then it doesn't meet the condition.)

    The first step is to find an offer that can give you the conversions you'll be needing to identify the best lander out of the ones you're split-testing. We'll go into how to split-test landers below. For now, know that we'll need some conversions, so we can see how many conversions each lander has made, in order to know which lander is the best one.

    And landers can't convert on their own without an offer. We want to find an offer that we can run with those landers to give us those conversions - AND that offer can't convert too badly, or it would spend too much money while collecting those conversions.

    Once you find that offer, pause all other offers in rotation, and just run that one offer to provide the conversions you'll be needing to identify the best lander.

    You may be wondering, "why 2 conversions?" It's just something that has worked for me. The first conversion is too arbitrary, i.e. the first offer to make the first conversion may have done so by luck. You may argue that 2 conversions aren't much better, and you may be right.

    If you feel that waiting until an offer has made 3 conversions before pausing the rest, please feel free to do so!

    But know that our goal isn't to pick the BEST-performing offer out of the bunch at this point - we just want an offer that converts well enough, so that we don't break the bank while collecting conversions to identify the best lander.

    After the best lander is identified (more on that later), we should re-test those offers we paused from before, and use statistics to determine a true winner.

    What if you have more than one offers that have reached 2 conversions or more? In that case, just pick the one that has made the most conversions. If they've both made the same 2 conversions, just pick one arbitrarily, and pause the rest.


    Action Implementation:

    To check how many conversions each offer has made: In Voluum, drill down in to "Offer" stats for your campaign.

    Remember to either update your tracker costs manually, or check the actual spend at the traffic source and figure out how much money each offer has spent. E.g. If actual spend is $12 and you're rotating 3 offers with equal weight, then each offer has spent $4.

    To pause an offer: In Voluum, go into the campaign's edit mode, click on the "DESTINATION" tab, click on the "Weight" field of the offer and change it from "100" to "0". Then "Save".





    *****

    Eliminate Dud Offers


    Condition to Check For: See whether any of the offers has spent 10x payout without converting at least once, OR has a conversion rate of less than one conversion per 10x payout in spend.

    Action to Take: Pause it from the tracker.

    It is in my experience that if an offer doesn't convert even once per 10x payout in spend (assuming you're using popular landers that are well-optimized), the chances of making profits with it are so small, it won't be worth running further.

    For smaller geos, you don't actually need to cut at 10x payout - you can cut earlier. This is because the smaller the geo, the less traffic is available, and the LESS you can afford to cut (placements etc.) to get green, and therefore the BETTER your offer will need to convert. But this type of judgement will require experience. You'll make better decisions as you launch and optimize more campaigns.

    In the meantime, the 10x payout rule will serve you well.

    If all of the offers you're testing now are eliminated by this rule, check Adplexity again to make sure you're using popular landers that have received lots of traffic and that many affiliates are using, and confirm that you've optimized them fully - i.e. to load fast, function correctly, and display well.

    And if you don't feel landers are the bottleneck, test another batch of offers. If you can't find more offers to test with the existing landers, start all over again - either select another sweepstakes prize or test another offer vertical or even another geo, and prepare a new set of landers and go from there.


    Action Implementation:

    Covered in the previous section.



    *****

    Cut Landers Down to the Winner


    ONLY do this AFTER you've found a good-enough offer to use for cutting landers - as described above.

    Condition to Check For: Every time you check your stats, enter lander stats into the stats calculator, and look for inferior lander(s) that have a "probability of being best" that falls below 10% when compared to the best lander.

    Action to Take: Pause the lander(s) from the tracker.

    Read this thread to learn how to enter lander stats into the stats calculator to cut inferior landers:

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...Banners-Part-1

    Do this regularly to cut inferior landers, until you're left with one winner.

    If more than one lander keeps going "neck-to-neck", i.e. are performing around the same, instead of spending money on identifying a winner, just pick a winner at random.

    IMPORTANT: When entering conversions into the stats calculator, you will need to use ONLY conversions that have resulted from the ONE offer you've chosen to use (i.e. the first one to reach 2 conversions).


    Action Implementation:

    In Voluum, for your campaign, drill down first by "Offer" and then by "Lander". Then, expand stats for the offer you've chosen (the first one to reach 2 conversions), and enter lander stats into the stats calculator as instructed in the tutorial linked to above, with "Visits" (i.e. impressions) as "Trials" and "Conversions" as "Successes".



    To pause an inferior lander: In Voluum, go into the campaign's edit mode, click on the "DESTINATION" tab, click on the "Weight" field of the lander and change it from "100" to "0". Then "Save".

    *****

    Cut Unpromising Placements


    Condition to Check For: In tracker, sort placements stats by cost in descending order. Identify placements that are in loss by 2x payout or more.

    Action to Take: Cut/blacklist the placement(s) at the traffic source.

    We need to check placement stats regularly to pause - aka "blacklist" - the ones that aren't converting well. There are lots of approaches to cutting placements. In this section, I'm providing you with a simple one you can start with.

    Before you're able to identify a good-enough offer you can use to cut landers, cut placements that are in loss by 2x or more, the payout of the lowest-payout offer.

    After you've chosen the offer for use in cutting landers, cut placements that are in loss by 2x or more, the payout of the chosen offer.

    (In any case, cut based on the placement's TOTAL cost across ALL offers, not the cost of the placement for the chosen offer.)

    Why 2x? Because in my experience, placements that are in loss by that much, have less of a chance to "catch up" to result in profits.

    You can of course wait until a placement is in loss by 3x, or more. You'd be more accurate, i.e. more-certain that the placement is an underperformer, thus minimizing chances of cutting a potentially profitable placement - but you'd also be spending more test budget.

    In fact, for larger geos that have a ton of traffic from a ton of placements, I would suggest to cut placements that don't convert within the first 1x payout in spend, or even the first 0.5x payout. Doing so can drastically lower the amount of money you need to bleed in the beginning. With such aggressive cutting, only the best placements will be left. If/Once the campaign is profitable, you can always turn some/all of the blacklisted placements back on to test further (preferably in batches so your campaign remains profitable).

    In order to see how much in loss each placement is, you'll need to know that placement's revenue and cost. Only then can you calculate the profit/loss.


    Action Implementation:

    There are several options when it comes to reviewing revenue and cost data, in deciding which placements to cut:


    1)Manually update campaign costs in the tracker, as described in the previous lesson. Then drill down into placement stats (this will be different for each traffic source; for PropellerAds it's "zoneid", and for PopAds it's "Website ID", assuming you added traffic source parameters using Voluum's default templates when adding these sources to Voluum). Click on "Profits" twice to sort by descending losses.



    Then identify placements that are in loss by 2x payout or more, then go to the traffic source to blacklist them.

    For PopAds: In PopAds, find your campaign and click on "Quick Menu" > "Website Targeting", select "Excluded", enter the website ids of the placements in the text box (one per line), and click "Save Changes".



    For PropellerAds: In PropellerAds, find your campaign and go into edit mode, find the "Zone limitation" section, select "Exclude", enter the website ids of the placements in the text box (either as separate lines or as a comma-separated list), and click "Save Changes". (There's another way to exclude zones in PropellerAds - as will be described in another section below.)



    This method is the simplest, but the least accurate assuming you're bidding SmartCPM at the traffic source. This is because when you bid SmartCPM, you're paying different rates for different placements - but when you manually update tracker costs at the campaign level, the tracker would have no way of knowing what the different rates are.

    Voluum would merely adjust each placement's cost based on the assumption that the CPM of each placement is equal to the overall/average campaign CPM, which makes the cost per placement inaccurate.


    2)Open in separate browsers windows, Voluum's placement stats and the traffic source's placement stats, sort both by descending impressions/visits. Look at both sets of data for EACH placement, and mentally calculate the profit/loss for that placement and decide whether or not to cut it.

    In Voluum, drill into placement stats and sort by descending "Visits" (which it should be by default):




    For PropellerAds: In PropellerAds go to "Dashboard" (left sidebar), set the time range to include all stats from beginning to now, find the row for your campaignand click on the chevron under "Show Stats"



    And you should see a list of placements/zones sorted by impressions in descending order (again, "Impressions" = "Visits" in Voluum):



    When sorted in this way, the placements in the 2 sets of stats (Voluum & PropellerAds) should be listed in about the same order, which makes it easier to cross-reference. You would then need to look at revenue stats in Voluum and then cost stats in PropellerAds, mentally calculate the profits/loss of each placement and figure out which ones to cut, and cut them by clicking on the "Exclude" link for the placement in the PropellerAds stats:




    For PopAds: In PopAds click on "Reports" in the left sidebar. Leave "Template" at default (I don't think it matters), set "Date and Time Range" to "Lifetime" (in which case you won't have to change "Timezone" or "Week Start"). In "Additional Filtering" > "Campaigns", select your campaign and click "Add >>". In "Group and Ordering" use the settings in the screenshot below:



    Then click on "Generate Report" and you'll see placement stats:



    Again, you would then cross-reference revenue stats in Voluum and cost stats in PopAds to make cutting decisions. To cut a placement, click on the "Exclude" link for that placement as shown in the image above.

    This method will show you accurate stats, but will require more work.


    3)Export placement stats from both Voluum and the traffic network, then use a spreadsheet to combine revenue stats from Voluum and cost stats from the traffic network. so you can see at a glance, accurate revenue and cost stats for each placement. You can find that spreadsheet here:

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...pampamp-easily

    Instructions on how to use it are included in that post.

    To export placement stats from Voluum: Drill down to placement stats for your campaign, then click on "Export" > "Download CSV":



    To export placement stats from PopAds: Remember we covered how to generate placement stats via "Report"? Once the report is generated, you'll see the option "Download Report" > "Excel Spreadsheet" located above the placement stats:



    To do the same in PropellerAds: Likewise, after displaying placement stats as described above, click on the "Export to CSV" link:




    So which of the 3 methods should you use? That would depend on how accurately you want to cut placements. I would just use method 1 in the beginning to save time. Then if/once the campaign is in profit, I MAY re-examine placement stats using the more time-consuming but more-accurate method 2 or 3 to see if I've cut out some placements prematurely - if so, I MAY revive those placements to run them further.

    But then, I'm lazy, which you can choose not to be. To me, the kind of effort required for methods 2 or 3 is only worth it for the bigger placements, and/or when running in a large geo where inaccuracies across a ton of placements can add up to a significant total difference.



    *****

    Test Paused Offers Plus Any New Ones


    Condition to Check For: You've just finished cutting down to the last lander (i.e. the winner).

    Action to Take: Test the offers that were paused (if any) when that first offer reached 2 conversions. And also any other offers you can find that can be tested using the winner lander. Split-test these offers with your current offer, and cut down to a winner.

    Also: The same rule as in the "eliminating dud offers" section above, also applies here. If any offer fails to convert at least once per 10x payout in spend, take it out.

    So you were able to find an offer with decent conversion rate, and kept running traffic until you had enough conversions to cut down to the last, winning lander.

    It is possible that at this point, the offer+lander combination you're running, may already be good enough to make a large-enough portion of the total traffic profitable - to the point where you can focus on cutting the unprofitable traffic to end up with a profitable campaign.

    However, even assuming your current offer+lander to be good enough, it would STILL be wise to test more offers at this point.

    Reason? A good offer can make ALL the difference to your campaign. You've already tested several landers and identified a winner, but as far as offers go, you've only really just used a single offer (not counting the ones you've paused in the beginning - because they probably didn't receive enough traffic to prove themselves either way).

    Moreover, you now have a winner lander that's proven to convert, so subsequent testing would be cheaper. You've nailed down a good lander, so the next step would be to nail down a good offer.

    In other words: Even if your current offer is good enough to yield a green campaign, it would be a shame not to test more offers at this point, simply because the potential of drastically improving campaign performance greatly outweighs the time and money this testing will require.

    So what you do is start a new offer split-test (using the winner lander) that includes:

    -The other offers you started testing, but put on pause after the first offer reached 2 conversions.

    -Any additional offers you may have on your offers log spreadsheet that can be run with this winner lander.

    -Any additional offers your AM can provide - new offers that don't yet have track record, or old offers that have received very little traffic from other affiliates. Unlike in the beginning when you needed offers that were proven to convert, you can now test any offer - because you now have a proven lander (so can avoid the catch-22 situation previously described).

    -If you have trouble finding more offers to test with your winner lander, you may want to sign up to more affiliate networks. Spy on Adplexity, check Offervault.com, ask here in the forum for good offers (and sometimes aff network reps will recommend them) - to see which aff networks have these offers and sign up for them. (Warning: If cashflow is an issue for you, try not to run with too many aff networks - having all your money stuck across many network accounts, with amounts that haven't reached minimum payment threshold, is not fun. So be selective and choose wisely! For example if you're planning on running sweeps for the next while, then join networks that focus on sweeps offers.)

    -Lastly, you need to include your current offer in the split-test, to see whether any of these new offers can "beat" it.


    The goal is to run this split-test and cut these offers down to a winner - just like what you did when split-testing landers!

    (NOTE: If you aren't able to find at least one offer to start a new split-test, read over the rest of this lesson anyway, then proceed to the next lesson.)


    Action Implementation:

    First of all, you would add the new offers to the offers spreadsheet (if they're not in there already), then add them as new offers to Voluum (if they haven't been already), and lastly, add them to the Voluum campaign (by going into campaign edit).

    It's important to make a note of the date and hour you're starting this new split-test (use the campaign journal), because when assessing stats using the stat calculator, we will ONLY use stats that are collected AFTER the split-test starts. All previous data - including previous stats of the current offer and winner lander - will need to be excluded.

    (When split-testing ANYTHING in general, all test candidates need to be exposed to the same set of conditions - thus they need to be run at the same time. Your campaign may receive different traffic quality from different placements etc. during different times, due to changes in competition levels etc. Therefore, you can't for example compare the performance of your current offer yesterday, to that of a new offer you started running today. That wouldn't be a fair comparison - say for example the current offer seemingly converts worse than the new offer, the reason could be that yesterday was a bad day for conversions - so we wouldn't be able to draw any conclusions.)

    It may be easier to just put the campaign on pause for the rest of the day (i.e. pause it at the traffic source), and start the split-test the next day - just so you wouldn't need to specify the "hours" in addition to the date range when checking stats.

    Next - we used a stats calculator to compare lander stats when doing a lander split-test. We need to do the same when comparing offer stats for this offer split-test, but because offers can have different payouts, we need to use another tool. This thread contains all the instructions:

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...211#post289211

    Also: Don't forget to cut offers that are converting worse than once per 10x payout in spend.

    To see offer stats in Voluum: Drill down into offer stats, and remember to specify the starting date and time as the time when you started this offer split-test.



    If you've put the campaign on pause for the day, then started traffic again the next day, then you can just choose "custom date range", then specify the starting date of the split-test and the current date to define the range.

    If you just kept running traffic and added the next offers into rotation, you'd need to choose "custom time range", then specify the starting date AND hour of the split-test. For the end of the range you can specify tomorrow's date and leave the hour at the default 0:00 - as long as the current time is included you're good.

    And we've already gone over how to cut offers in Voluum somewhere above - you're all set!

    Keep cutting until you're left with the last, winning offer. (Note: Occasionally, you'll have more than one offer in the end that are performing the same, i.e. "going neck-to-neck", and it doesn't look like statistical significance would be met anytime soon, then just pick the one with the higher revenue to continue.)



    *****

    Lander and Offer Testing Checkpoint

    Generally, one of two outcomes will result from the process described in this lesson:


    1)None of the offers you tested passed the "reach 2 conversions with conversion rate of once per 10x payout or better" test.

    What to do in this case:

    Start testing from scratch again by replacing your offers with new ones.

    You may want to look for the same type of offer that can be used with your current set of landers, so you wouldn't "waste" them.

    You may need to sign up to more affiliate networks to get more offers - spy on Adplexity, check Offervault.com, ask here in the forum for good offers (and sometimes aff network reps will recommend them) - to see which aff networks have these offers and sign up for them.

    Alternatively, you can choose to run another vertical - in which case you'd need to rip new landers etc. - basically start from scratch. (Check Adplexity to make sure you choose a vertical that's doing volume, which is an indication that there may be good offers for this geo+vertical. Try digging into Adplexity to find out which aff networks has these offers.)

    Lastly, you could even just try another geo.


    2)You found an offer with decent conversion rate, used that to cut landers down to a winner, then (if you were able to find more offers) used the winning lander to test more offers to cut down to a winner.

    What to do in this case:

    You'll need to assess at this point, whether the campaign is likely to become profitable with further optimization.

    Again: If your winning lander + winning offer combination is able to make enough of the traffic profitable, then you can tweak your targeting until the campaign is profitable (e.g. by blacklisting unprofitable traffic and/or whitelisting profitable traffic and/or testing bids etc.). We'll be covering optimization in the next few lessons.

    If your "winning lander + winning offer" are NOT good-enough, then you'd need to keep testing landers and offers. For example split-test another round of landers - where you can test variations of your winning lander and even completely new custom designs created by you. (Note: Because you already have a proven offer+lander that converts at least semi-well, you can now test unproven landers without fear of the catch-22 scenario.) Or split-test another round of offers (again, can be unproven).

    Or even start from scratch and test another vertical for that geo, or test another geo.

    (Actually, even for the "not-good-enough" cases, there's one last hope: Testing bids. We'll be covering that in one of the next lessons.)

    *****

    Regarding Test Budget, and Running in he Same Geos Repeatedly

    Notice that throughout this lesson, I haven't once mentioned test budget.

    I've decided not to limit you to a certain test budget recommendation, simply because there are too many different possible scenarios.

    Pop camps used to be easier to run. It was easier to suggest a test budget - for example I could tell you something like, "if you run in a tier 3/4 geo, spend 30-50x average offer payout on optimized landers and AM-recommended offers, and not see promise, you should probably test another vertical, or geo." (For example.)

    I used to suggest for new affiliates to test lots of geos using small budgets and picking promising ones to focus on. With the increase competition, this is not AS feasible these days.

    Instead, I would suggest for you to invest money and effort into every geo you run, so that you can rise above the competition - by testing more landers than they do, more offers than they do, and investing into cutting placements and testing bids and other optimization activities more so than they do. Basically try to conquer the geo.

    I don't want you to give up on a geo after spending an arbitrary budget. (But at the same time, I've put in the "1 conversion per 10x payout in spend or better" rule to avoid your having to spend a ridiculous amount of money on testing - and that's the WORST case scenario because you'll get better at cutting offers earlier than that as you gain more experience.)

    Moreover, I would suggest for you to run in the same geos REPEATEDLY to benefit from your initial investment again and again. Here are some of the benefits:

    -You'd already know what the best placements are, so you could set up a campaign and target those placements only (an approach known as "whitelisting"), which will save you money when testing new landers and offers. (Once the campaign is profitable, you can always test other placements!)

    -You'd already have a proven lander (and maybe even a proven offer, if it's still active on the aff network) in one or more verticals, that you can use to quickly test new offers.

    -If you've tested lander variations or custom landers, you'd understand what kinds of marketing tactics or psychological triggers the people in that geo responds well to. You may already know which hours of the day and/or days of the week typically convert the best in that geo and one or more verticals, and test new offers during those hours/days to save money. (Again, you can always test other hours/days once the campaign becomes profitable.)


    In short: When you have some success with a certain geo, you should keep seeking out new offers to test there (same vertical as or different vertical from the initial camp you run there).

    Make the initial investment, then reap the benefits repeatedly. Doing so will make you more efficient in terms of time and budget, and give you a leg-up on the competition.



    *****

    Date & Time the Winner Offer+Lander Emerged

    When you cut down to the best offer+lander, be sure to make a note of of the DATE and TIME you made your last cut, in the campaign journal!

    Remember Voluum only allows to drill down 3 levels deep (i.e. specify 3 variables)? From this point onwards, you'll mainly be looking at stats produced by the winning lander+offer. So if you're looking at lifetime stats and want to see, say, OS stats, you'd have to drill down into Offer > Lander > OS (and expand OS stats for the winning lander+offer), and this would already be 3 levels deep. If you want to drill down further into OS > Device for example, you won't be able to, because that would be more than 3 levels.

    However, if you set a custom time range in Voluum, and set the start of the date+hour range to the date+hour when you cut your last offer/lander to leave only the current winning offer+lander combo, then the resulting stats would only involve the current winning offer+lander combo. THEN you can drill down to the variables directly without having to first drill down into Offer > Lander. (e.g. You can drill down to OS > Device > Model, without first having to drill down to Offer > Lander - because ALL stats that are displayed would already be for your winning lander+offer - since you've cut all other landers and offers before that custom time range.)

    Hey Kash,

    I´m really sorry but I don´t really get what you mean with "if I promote page A" and "from which website I would get paid" so I try to explain you the basic construct so that it hopefully helps to answer your quesiton.

    You don´t really promote a page, you promote offers that you find in CPA networks.

    And these CPA networks are also the ones who pay you.

    Basically your campaigns consist of three different blocks:

    The trafficsource, your own infrastructure and the CPA networks.

    The first block is the trafficsource, this is where you buy your traffic from.

    When we use your post as an example then the trafficsource woulkd be Propeller Ads.

    The trafficsources basically connect your campaigns to many different websites where you then buy traffic from through the interface of the trafficsource.

    The traffic that you buy will be sent to your tracker, and that´s when we arrive the second block, your own infrastructure.

    Your own infrastructure is your tracker (from your post it would be Voluum) and your landing page, these things are directly connected so that you can track exactly from where you received your traffic, how much you paid for it and what converts and what not.

    So the first step when a user arrives at your infrastructure is a through the campaignlink that you used in the trafficsource.

    Through this link the user gets redirected to your tracker and the tracker receives information like which website sent the user and how much you paid for it.
    Then the tracker sends the user to your landing page.

    When the user clicks through from your landing page to the offers he gets redirected through your tracker again so that the tracker exactly knows which users click through and which not.

    In this step the user leaves your infrastructure and goes to the last block, the CPA network.

    The CPA networks have the offers that you want to promote and they will also send data back to your tracker when a conversion happens.

    To make that happen you have to use a postback URL that you set in the CPA networks.

    When your tracker then redirects a user from your landing page to the offer there is a string added to the offer URL, the click ID.

    The click ID is unique for each user and when a conversion happens then the CPA network sends the click ID through the postback to your tracker.

    And because the click ID is unique the tracker can show you exactly which user converted, which placement sent the user, how much you paid for it, how much the user made you and all such stuff.

    So you buy traffic from websites through the trafficsources, you use landing pages to promote offers that you get from CPA networks and the CPA networks then pay you for the conversions on these offers.

    I hope that helps, if not feel free to ask

    What you refer to as impressions are most probably visits in Voluum (you're running pop campaigns, right?).

    In Voluum, impression tracking is something else - it is meant for campaigns where a visitor first sees an ad (1 impression = 1 ad loading on a page) and then, clicks on a link and is redirected to a landing page. So the flow is: ad -> landing page -> offer page.
