    Day 27: Introduction to Offer+Lander Testing

    **********************************

    ACTION & EXPLANATION

    From this point onwards, I'll probably continue to include ACTION & EXPLANATION in the same section - because we've ventured into processes that are more complicated, that will involve decision-making. I want to include explanations alongside suggested actions so that you'd know how to make good decisions.



    A Word on Testing & Optimization Strategy



    There is NOT a single correct way to test offers and landers, or to optimize a campaign.

    Every campaign is different. There are so many variables that I can't possibly give you a one-size-fits-all testing and optimization strategy that will work every time (and it's not due to lack of trying on my part).

    What I WILL strive to do, is provide a general testing and optimization strategy that is simple enough for a brand new affiliate to execute, and yet effective enough to help you find your first profits.

    Then, once you're familiar with this general strategy, you can read other good threads on pop campaigns - follow-alongs, case-studies, etc. - to pick up additional tips and tricks that you can build into the general strategy. (I will also try to share or link to some of these in a future lesson for your reference.)

    Moreover, as you gain experience, your strategy will naturally evolve from my general strategy anyway. And the more experience you have, the earlier you can tell whether a campaign is worth pursuing or not, the earlier you will stop unpromising campaigns, and the fewer promising campaigns you'll abandon prematurely by mistake.

    Overall, the more experience you have, the better decisions you'll make when faced with different situations. Nothing can replace actual experience, but I will do my best to shorten that learning curve in the remaining lessons in this tutorial.



    General Offer+Lander Testing Strategy

    Here I'm going to elaborate on what I said in a previous lesson - the "First Lesson on Testing and Optimization".

    In the beginning, when you're setting up a campaign for a new geo+vertical for the first time, you're basically flying blind because you don't have any previous stats to compare to.



    Remember the 3 pillars of the pop campaign that I've mentioned before? i.e. Offer, Lander, and Traffic. All 3 factors need to at least be of decent quality in order to get a reasonably good conversion rate.

    If even ONE of these factors is of bad quality, it will create a bottleneck, such that no matter how good the other 2 factors are, conversions will be limited.

    The really tricky part is that, when your new campaign doesn't generate conversions, it would be difficult to tell whether it's the OFFERs, LANDERs, or TRAFFIC QUALITY that's limiting the campaign. This catch-22 situation happens a lot more often than you may think - I see this in follow-alongs all the time.



    This is why, in the beginning, you need to do your best to ensure the quality of all 3 factors. This is how you do it, in a nutshell:

    TRAFFIC - Choose a traffic source that has good-quality traffic. Bid average or slightly higher to make sure the traffic source isn't sending you traffic that nobody else is bidding for (i.e. traffic from placements that don't convert).

    Doing a bot test first will help as well (more on that in the next lesson).

    OFFER - Start with offers that have some type of track-record, e.g. ones that are suggested by AMs as having converted well for at least a few other affiliates, or offers that seem to be doing well based on spy tool stats (e.g. in Adplexity, exhibiting an uptrend over a few days/weeks), etc.

    Also, try to test more than one offer in the campaign wherever possible - testing a few offers rather than just one will increase the campaign's chances of profitability.

    LANDER - Test at least 5 landers (again, testing a few rather than just one will increase your chances of reaching profits). Make sure they've received a lot of traffic (in Adplexity, sorted by "Received most traffic") over at least a few days (chances are, few people would send high traffic to a non-converting lander for long).

    Choose landers that look very different from each other if possible (to cast a bigger net - you can always test similar landers later on). Make sure you take time to optimize the landers: So that they display well in/on various devices and browsers, function correctly, and load fast.


    In previous lessons, you should already have chosen some offers and prepared some landers for your first campaign. In this lesson, we'll go over how to set up a campaign in Voluum to split-test those offers and landing pages. In the next few lessons, you'll see how the General Offer+Lander Testing Strategy is executed.

    In short, these are the steps involved:

    1)Set up a campaign in Voluum to rotate the offers and landers (what this lesson will cover).

    2)Set up a campaign at a proven traffic source like PropellerAds or PopAds to drive traffic.

    3)Ideally, one of the offers will reach 2 conversions first, with a conversion rate that isn't overly low (e.g. converts better than once per every 10x payout in traffic cost). If that's the case, pause all other offers and just keep running that one - to generate enough conversions for us to be able to tell which LANDER is converting the best out of the 5 or more landers in rotation.

    4)Once we have a WINNING LANDER, we'll assess the campaign to whether the current offer+lander combo is converting well-enough for us to rely on cutting placements etc. to push the campaign into profits. If so, we'll focus on cutting. If not, we'll need to test more offers and/or landers first to increase conversion rate, before focusing on cutting.
    IMPORTANT: Once we have an offer that converts at least semi-well, plus a lander that is a proven winner, we'll be able to test other offers and landers - even ones that don't have good track-record - such as offers that are new to an affiliate network, or custom landers you've designed yourself from scratch.

    You'll no longer run into the catch-22 situation mentioned above. Let's say you want to test a new offer - if you don't get conversions, you'll KNOW the new offer is a dud - because you're already using a proven winning lander. The same applies to new landers - because you're using an offer that has generated conversions in the past when run with popular landers, if the camp stops generating conversions you'd know your new landers are not-so-good.

    The same also applies to traffic quality. Once you have a proven offer+lander combination, you can test different bids and different targeting options.

    The idea of using proven factors at first to avoid the catch-22 situation is a very important one. I hope I've explained this concept clearly. I'll go into the details on HOW to execute the testing and optimization in this and the next few lessons.



    How to Set Up a Multi-Offer Multi-Lander Campaign in Voluum


    Step 1: Add Offers to Voluum

    We've gone over how to do this in a previous lesson. Remember to use FINAL links (including clickid, campaign id and traffic source id - you should have constructed these final links in your offers spreadsheet before this).

    You'll notice the rotation weights after each offer:



    If you run the campaign as it is, with the weight of both offers set to 100% by default, it will rotate through all the specified offers evenly. For example if you add 2 offers, approximately 50% of the visitors will be shown the first offer and the other 50% will see the second offer. In a vast majority of cases, rotating equally through all split-test candidates would be ideal, so you won't need to temper with the rotation weight.

    OPTIONAL: Voluum has a "Traffic Distribution AI" feature that will automatically shift more weight to offers that are getting more conversions.



    I would strongly recommend that you leave this setting disabled (which it is by default) for now. Try to learn to optimize everything manually first. Once you know what you're doing, you can implement automation - otherwise you'd have little understanding on how the optimization process works. When you're ready to give this feature a test though, here's more info on how it works:

    https://doc.Voluum.com/en/traffic_distribution_ai.html

    And here's a thread where members are sharing their experiences with this feature:

    Step 3: Create Campaign in Voluum

    -In Voluum go to "Campaign" -> "+New campaign" (-> "New TRK Campaign" if you've also signed up to Voluum DSP).

    -For "Campaign destination" choose "Path". Under "Landers" click on "1. Select lander", and select one of your landers. To add the rest of the landers, click on "Add lander" for however many additional landers you're wanting to add, then click on each entry and specify the lander.

    -Under "Offers" add your offers for the chosen geo+vertical+prize, the same way you added the landers. (Please see the next section on how many offers to add.)



    -Leave other settings at default. Don't forget to click "Save"!

    ADDITIONAL EXPLANATIONS

    There are several other things I want to elaborate on, that will help you.



    How Many Offers to Test?

    Preferably 2-5.

    However, if you can only find one offer for a given geo+prize combination (assuming you're running sweeps), then feel free to just test the one. I would try to test offers where I could find more than one offer for the same geo+prize first, though.

    Or, in the opposite situation where you have more than 5 offers for a given geo+prize, feel free to test some or all of them at the same time. Basically the more offers you include, the greater the chance of finding a good one, but the more stats you'll need to collect to know which offers are better/best.

    You can test them all at the same time, or test them in batches, with each batch in a separate split-test.

    Example: Say you have 10 offers and you want to test them in batches of 3-4. You would split-test offers #1-3 first - let's say offer #3 wins. Then you'd start a new split-test with #3 and #4-6 - let's say #5 wins. Then you'd start another split-test with #5 and #7-10 to arrive at the final winner.

    For this first campaign, just add 2-5 offers (or just test one offer if you absolutely can't find another one for any geo+prize combo). For future campaigns you're encouraged to test different approaches to see which one suits you.



    How to Handle Offers that Accept Different Traffic?

    If all the offers you've chosen for the geo+vertical (and if it's a sweepstakes offer, then also +prize) accept the same carriers OSs, browsers etc., then it would be straight forward - just add them all to the campaign as we did above.

    But what if each offer accepts different carriers, OSs, devices, browsers etc.? How would you set that up?

    There are a number of options. I'll show you what's possible first, then show you my "lazy" way of handling this.

    First of all, to simplify things, here's Rule #1:

    Set up each campaign to target either a single carrier, or wifi. Add to each campaign the offers that accept traffic from that carrier/wifi.
    Example: Offer1 accepts CarrierX & CarrierZ; Offer2 accepts CarrierY & CarrierZ; Offer3 accepts CarrierX. We'd set up Campaign1 to run Offer1 and Offer3 and target CarrierX at the traffic source; Campaign2 to run Offer2 and target CarrierY at the traffic source; and Campaign3 to run Offer1 and Offer2, targeting CarrierZ at the traffic source.

    Note that this isn't a hard rule - if you're wanting to run a quick test by targeting all accepted carriers + wifi in a single campaign, just to see which carriers/wifi convert well-enough to warrant further testing, that would be a good approach as well.

    However, I would suggest sticking to this rule at the start to keep things simple. Targeting a single carrier/wifi per campaign has the following advantages:

    -It would eliminate the need to drill down into Voluum's carrier stats (as you know the entire campaign is targeting a certain carrier, or wifi). As Voluum allows drilling of only up to 3 levels deep, freeing up a slot will allow you to drill down into something else as needed.



    -Carrier traffic is more expensive than wifi traffic, and even traffic from different carriers will cost different. This means each carrier/wifi will have a different average bid. By targeting them in separate campaigns, you'll be able to bid appropriately for each carrier/wifi. Targeting multiple carriers/wifi in the same camp will result in overbidding of some and underbidding of others.

    I'll elaborate on the carrier vs. wifi topic at the end of this lesson - for now let's keep things simple. Moving on...

    So we've covered carriers. But what if you have offers that accept different OSs, device types, and browsers? This is where we need to do some planning.

    In order to send the right traffic to the right offers, we have 2 tools: 1)Voluum rules, and 2)Targeting at the traffic source.



    Voluum Rules Explained

    I'm going to introduce you to Voluum rules. Follow these steps to get some practice:

    -In Voluum, create a new campaign and name it "Voluum Rules Test". Click "Next", then choose "Path" as campaign destination.

    -Find the "Rule-based paths" section. Click on "+ Add rule", then click on "New rule".



    -For "Rule name" put "Android OS & Chrome Browser". Under "Conditions" click on "Device".



    -Under "Operating system and version" click "Add condition". Notice how, of the "Is" and "Is not" options, "Is" is highlighted by default.

    -In the left-most search box, search for and checkmark "Android". Notice all versions of Android are selected by default. (In future if you have offers that only accept certain OS versions, you can select them here.)



    -Scroll down to "Browser and version" and click "Add condition". Search for "chrome" and checkmark "Chrome" and "Chrome Mobile".



    -Then, back in the left hand side of the window, click on "New path" under your new rule:



    -This is where you can specify the landers and offers you're wanting to run - you can select random landers and offers as this is just a test. Click "Save".

    -What you just did, was set up a rule that said "if the visitor is using Android OS and Chrome Browser, run these offers and landers".

    -Now, for practice, set up another rule yourself that says "if the visitor is using IOS OS and Android Browser...". (Start by clicking on "Add rule".) Again, specify some random landers and offers.

    -Lastly, for the default path at the bottom, specify some random landers and offers.


    If you were to run this campaign now, the top rule will get executed first, then the one below it. After all the rules are executed, the "Default paths" section at the very bottom will get executed last.

    This is how Voluum rules are executed:

    -Say we were to send traffic to this campaign now. Every visitor that comes along, would be compared to the top rule first, which is "if the visitor is using Android OS and Chrome Browser, send them to one of these offers and landers."

    -If the visitor does not meet both criteria, they would be compared to the next rule, which is "if the visitor is using IOS OS and Android Browser, send them to one this other set of landers and offers".

    -And if the visitor still does not meet both of these criteria, they would be sent to the "Default Paths" at the bottom - to one of the offers and landers listed in that path.

    Although I've used 2 conditions for every rule in the example above, I very rarely use more than 1 condition, as I'll explain below. The above is just to give you an idea of what's possible.

    So the point is, whenever you're trying to split-test a bunch of offers that have different targeting requirements - OSs, Browsers, and Device Types being the common ones - you'll need to somehow use Voluum rules + targeting options at the traffic source to send the right traffic to the right offers.

    Voluum Rule Example 2

    In the last example, the offers were only different in ONE type of targeting, i.e. OSs. What if they were different in TWO types of targeting? For example, let's take the last example and add a DEVICE TYPE restriction:

    IF Operating system and version = Android AND Device Type = Tablet, run Offers 1, 2, 4

    IF Operating system and version = Windows Phone AND Device Type = Phone, run Offers 1, 3, 4

    IF Operating system and version = RIM AND Device Type = Phone, run Offer 3

    IF Device Type = Phone, run Offer 3

    Default Paths > Path 1. run Offer 4
    If you can't figure out this mess, don't bother. I mainly just want to illustrate 1)how creative you can get with Voluum rules, and 2)how messy things can get once you try to take care of more than one targeting type.



    Voluum Rules Simplified - How I Do It

    So you've noticed that having to set up conditions for 2 parameters - in our example OS and Device Type - can result in having many rules. Now imagine if the offers had restrictions in additional parameters - such as Browser requirements. It could get REALLY messy!

    To avoid all the trouble, I usually either use rules ONLY for OSs, or none at all. This is what I do:

    -For tier 3 and 4 geos, Android is the main OS that's responsible for most of the traffic anyways, so I would either just target Android OS at the traffic source when doing initial testing, or just target all OSs anyways even if certain offers don't accept a particular OS - not much traffic would be wasted anyway. In this case I wouldn't even set up Voluum rules.

    -For tier 1 and 2 geos, if the offers accept different OSs, I would sometimes go through the trouble of setting up Voluum rules for OSs, like in Example 1 above.

    -But having rules for OSs is as far as I will go. For Device Types: If some offers don't accept tablets, I would just target phones at the traffic source - there isn't much tablet traffic in comparison anyways. For Browsers: If some offers don't accept certain browsers, and if they are small browsers (e.g. Opera Mini), I would just not target it at the traffic source. If there are offers that have browser restrictions for big browsers, I would either just send the unaccepted traffic anyways (rather waste some traffic than messing around with rules) or just start a separate camp to test them.

    -Once a winning offer is found, I could then just set up targeting at the traffic source to cater to it. So for example if the offer also accepts IOS but I was just sending Android traffic to all offers before for sake of simplicity, and it turned out to be a winner, I would also add IOS traffic to it later.


    So to recap, when testing a bunch of offers, I would:

    1)Generally only target one geo and carrier in each camp when testing offers.

    2)I would set up Voluum rules for OSs for bigger geos, and tier 1/2 geos that have lots of traffic from OSs other than Android.     But that's as much trouble as I'd be willing to take.

    3)For OS restrictions in tier 3/4 geos, or for other target parameters for all geos such as device types and browsers and whatnot, I wouldn't bother setting up Voluum rules. I'd either send unaccepted traffic to certain offers, or just exclude from targeting at the traffic source. (Sometimes the advertiser will complain about the unaccepted traffic - in which case I'd just pause that offer and test it later.)
    You can choose to do this any way you like - this is just my lazy way for your reference.

    A More Efficient Way to Test Offers that Accept WIFI+CARRIER Traffic

    Remember I suggested to set up one campaign to target each carrier? There's actually a more efficient way that I use, to minimize test budget, but it only applies to offers that accept both wifi and carrier.

    1)If the conversion flow for both wifi and carrier are the same - let's say both are pin submits - then I would just set up a campaign to test wifi traffic first, and not set up camps to test carriers yet. This is because wifi traffic is cheaper, and if the offer doesn't perform well on wifi traffic, then chances are it won't perform significantly better on carrier traffic either (because conversion flow is the same).

    Of course, if the offer converts well on wifi traffic, you should set up a campaign to test it on carrier traffic as well.

    2)If the conversion flow for wifi and carrier are different, e.g. 1-click flow for carrier but pin submit for wifi, then I would test carriers first. My reasoning is that if the offer doesn't convert reasonably well for the simpler conversion flow, then chances are it would only convert worse on the more-complicated flow. You can set up one campaign per carrier. Or, if you want to save time, target all accepted carriers in a single campaign, but this is just to get an initial idea on which carrier has the most potential - once you see one or more carriers looking hopeful, you would want to set up separate campaigns to target each carrier anyway. Traffic from different carriers cost different so it would be best to target one at a time. Another reason would be that Voluum only allows drilling down to 3 levels, so if you're only targeting one carrier, you don't need to drill down into "carrier", but instead free up that slot for drilling down into other data.

    Of course, if the offer converts well on carrier traffic, you should set up a campaign to test it on wifi traffic as well.

    The tokens for the lander URL are used to display information on the landing page itself. For example, the LP wants to call out the users city location, you will need Voluums token for that appended to the lander URL. Without it, wherever the code for the lander calls the users location, it will not show up. (As well as needing to have the proper syntax in the lander code for it). If you see GetURLParameters in your lander code, figure out where on the lander it is calling it (to see if it makes sense to even be there in the first place) and append the token name onto the end of the lander URL. It's possible your lander has code for getURLParameter stuff, but it's not actually being used anywhere on the lander, in this case you wouldn't need to append it to the URL. Don't mix the lander URL tokens with Voluums reporting, that info is brought in from the traffic source. In this tutorial, when we added propellerads and popads, we loaded them in with templates. Those template tokens is what brings in the info to your camp, which then helps Voluum report off of (someone correct me if I'm wrong please).

    To answer your question, is it still necessary? It depends, as I explained above, you'll need to go through your code and see if any getURLParameter info is being called on to your lander. If it is, you'll need to append the tokens onto the Lander URL.

    You mean create a separate campaign for every creative? That would be so much work.

    You can have multiple push ad creatives per campaign, the trick is to track their IDs so you know which creatives convert.

    Make sure you have your traffic source's parameter for ad creative set up in advanced parameters in traffic source settings in Voluum. This way you'll be passing creative's ID to Voluum for every visitor. Then, group a campaign report by this variable and check how different creatives are performing.
