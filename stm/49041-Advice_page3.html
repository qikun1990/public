    Ok understood about the high payout offers, how would one approach it? Would making a credible lander and buying traffic on propellorads work in your experience?
    For these expensive products, it's the best to use some source that allows better targeting. So FB would be the perfect match, BUT since these verticals are not always whitehat as you mentioned... it's hard to run them on FB... all the people I know used to cloak. The more clean offers can be promoted on native sources.

    POP and Push sources would likely approve them, but the conversions would be pretty low. I see quite a bit of crypto offers promoted on push though, these seem to perform ok.

    Yes building a blog is definitely something worth putting time in it seems like. But, how to get traffic to that blog/site? And if in the future if have a blog or site and if we promote weight loss or nutra (which are not all completely whitehat), will it affect the blog and cause the viewers to resent the blog? And how would one be able to rank the site higher in google? (Is that a paid thing or a keyword thing?)
    Blogs rely on organic traffic from the most part... so SEO + sharing content on social media are the 2 most important things to focus on. You can jumpstart the traffic with some paid ads too, for example to try to make a social media post go viral or to reach more readers for an article that you think is really perfect and might bring in some loyal readers. You can also use paid services in order to get backlinks or have your blog/site properly optimized... but in the beginning I'd recommend to do it manually in order to learn the process.

    Promoting scammy products on your blog can definitely affect your user base and upset people, so if you decide to dedicate all that time to building a page and growing it's traffic, it would also be a good idea to chose the products wisely. I'm sure there are at least 'decent" nutra/weightloss products out there that you could promote. And there are many products that actually provide value to people who want to lose weight... courses or guides, recipe books, fitness gear etc...

    Template: there are thousands of them my man, both free and paid, you need to choose one yourself based on what functionality you plan to use and what you want it to look like.

    Here are the 2 biggest, I believe, template resources: https://www.templatemonster.com/ and https://themeforest.net/ Both of them have 1000s of templates listed.

    Most blogs are using wordpress as their CMS, I'd go with that.

    should I be posting on both
    One of the SEO rules that was important, when I was still into it, was: if you are about to post the same content on multiple sources, always make sure you post it first on your OWN page. You want to give google some time to "discover" the content on your own page first, so when it finds it on other sources later on, it doesn't consider the one on your site a duplicate. So I would start by posting it on your blog first.

    But, since you're not about to post the full piece on FB or Insta or any other social network, it probably doesn't matter much... the rule was more for guest posting etc. So for example, if one of your strategies would be to write an article and post in on medium.com, you should do this after the original article was already crawled by google from your own blog, know what I mean?

    In reality, you still need to post the article on your blog first, then announce it on social media as you need to have the article online in order to be able to link to it in the first place, right?

    So for driving traffic to the blog/FB, should throw money into SEO and FB/IG advertising?
    You can start by boosting the FB posts where you link to the blog article. Alternatively, you can look at native networks and promote the best articles there, if you manage to find the right widgets, it could work well.

    But your main focus should be organic growth and for that you need quality content. SO focus on keyword research first, then write good content that matches the keywords you want to target and make sure you're well optimized for google. Links are still a very important part of SEO, so do some reading on how to build them and dedicate some time to it as well.

    Yes competition is tough but I wonder sometimes whether that means that it is a good thing?

    What I am perhaps trying to ask is that are there any affiliate marketing areas in which the competition is quite small or non-existent?
    Well, see it that way.

    Mostly it all comes down to the offer.

    Advertisers create offers for the geos where they can make most money.

    This means that for specific geos or targetings there are just more and better offers available, it can also differ a bit from vertical to vertical.

    In these combinations where the good offers are available you will have higher competition but also more and better offers available.

    Nonetheless there are geos or targetings with low or almost no competition, when you then find good offers there you basically have what you are looking for but the chances to find good offers in such an environment are lower than finding good converting offers in geos or targetings where many good offers are available.

    Both approaches have their advantages and disadvantages.

    There are many who do have teams though, no doubt about that, what Im trying to say is that having a team is not a MANDATORY part of the success recipe.

    When talking about these big guns, you need to realize the costs of operation. Every employee equals to an expense they have to pay so they have to bring in the revenue to justify that. Running campaigns in small tier 2-3 geos with limited profitability simply doesn't make sense here, they'd be losing money. That's why they tend to target large tier1 geos, where the profit potential is way higher. There are exceptions of course, some of these lower tier countries have so many inhabitants that they still can bring in a lot of revenue with the right offers. But the smaller ones tend to be ignored by large affiliates with teams, that's why we always recommend to target these GEOs to new affiliates.

    What I am perhaps trying to ask is that are there any affiliate marketing areas in which the competition is quite small or non-existent? Can we create a new form of affiliate marketing or a new vertical/niche? Or are we stuck in this system and will forever have to compete with the sharks in the same pool that are much greater than us in which victory seems slim to none.
    Competition is usually small when something new emerges. When carrier billing started, it was extremely easy to make money with mobile traffic. When push traffic started, it was very easy to profit from it... and there is always something new, but it comes with a learning curve and the will to experiment. These days there is tiktok which is growing and it's not that widely used as an ad network yet... snapchat isnt saturated still either, pinterest is another one. There is the resurrected in-page push format growing right now. There is always something "new" and if it's not, it will emerge sooner or later.

    And yes, you can also create your own market... identify some need, provide the solution and create an offer for it, then promote it on the traffic networks out there. People are spending more and more $$$ online, there is always way to get a slice of it. Affiliate marketing can work in any vertical or niche, the skills are pretty much universally transferable, you don't have to stick with what the standard networks offer. Info products are booming, look at udemy and how much it has grown... maybe you have a skill that other might want to master?

    Try to think outside of the box, the options are limitless

    You've only been running a single lander? If so then you'd need to test more landers than that!

    You're implying this question yourself: When conversion rate is shitty, do you blame the traffic, the offer or the lander?

    -By including a few of the most popular landers (and well-optimized for speed etc.), you can be confident that at least one of them would be a good converter.

    -By using a network with good reputation (like PropellerAds so you're covered here), and bidding at least the average amount, you can be confident that the traffic quality is good.

    -By testing a few offers, you'd increase your chances of finding at least one good one. BUT - if you're only testing one offer, as long as you're confident in traffic quality and lander quality as stated above, THEN if the offer doesn't convert, you can be relatively sure that the offer's the issue.


    Again: There are 3 pillars - offers + landers + traffic. You need to be confident in AT LEAST two of those - otherwise if you don't get conversions you wouldn't be able to tell which is/are the culprit(s). Hope that's clear!
