    Hey there, sorry for the late reply.

    I wouldn't apply the 10x rule to landers - that would be way too big a spend (i.e. 40x payout for 4 landers).
    Well, I still would apply the rule but I also use some kind of "safety net".

    When I start a test round and don´t see any or only 1 conversion after 50% of test budget for a lander I would take the lander out of the rotation, adjust budget and continue.
    When I don´t see any real signes to succeed after 50% of the test budget for the whole test I stop it and test something else.

    That way you never risk to lose all your budget, the test only rans until 10x payout when there are any conversions at all.

    Probably I should have been a bit clearer about it.
    Sorry for that.

    And of course the formula won´t work for each and every campaign you run, just imagine using it when you test 5 different Crypto FTD offers with $600 payout each and 4 different landers.

    So it´s by far no cut in stone rule that is good for everything you run.

    But especially in the beginning it´s good to have at least a rough rule of thumb to use so that you get a somewhat steady process for your campaigns.

    This helps alot compared to running each campaign more or less random without a good structure.

    @manchester you're in great hands with @twinaxe and @jaybot throwing such valuable advice at the speed of light!

    Here are my thoughts:


    -Regarding small payout offers - they have their place and purpose - which I was just talking about in another post:

    https://stmforum.com/forum/showthrea...l=1#post388484

    HOWEVER, in your particular case, that offer converts way too badly to even use as a tripwire/detector.

    Nevertheless, something to keep in mind for the future.


    -Regarding lead quality: Although it IS true that misleading landers lead to lower lead quality (i.e. what visitors see is not what they get so they bail), honest landers usually don't convert too well either. And even if you don't run misleading angles, your competitors will. It's not easy trying to find a balance between the two and STILL turn a profit. Thus is the joy of running pop.


    -Regarding how to succeed with pop: It's not easy anymore, but it can be done.

    Mass-testing - but in an efficient manner so you don't lose your shirt - is key. If you have more time than money, test multiple geos (and even different types of offers across geos) at the same time. Run a limited amount of traffic in each to test the waters, then pick the most promising to test further.

    As to how to choose those geos and offer types, jaybot has made suggestions. You can also look at the top offers lists of all the networks you're working with, to see which offers and geos are hot. Looking through spy tools for campaigns that have run for a few days at decent volume would be another way.

    Once you have a good offer, try to scale to multiple traffic networks to see which networks have good traffic in that geo, and also to build those placement blacklists. The first time you do, you may end up reinvesting some/all of your profits. But if that offer lasts, or if you catch another good offer in the same geo next time, you'll have access to lots of traffic volume.

    Volume is KEY. Affiliate networks and managers would reward you with pay bumps, access to exclusive offers, or cap to the best offers. Experienced affiliates know this, and we would run offers at a minor loss JUST to demonstrate our ability to run volume.

    The reality of things is that unless you have the know-how to keep your landers out of the spy tools, everyone has access to your landers (in fact you yourself are ripping landers from others). As a newbie, you're running offers that other affiliates have access to, at street (i.e. default) payout. And most people are running on the same traffic sources, i.e. Propeller, PopAds, Zeropark...

    Which results in most affiliates running the same landers and same offers on the same traffic sources.

    So how to get a leg up? The good news is you don't have to beat out every other competitor. You just need to beat out some of them to get a slice of the pie.

    Test more landers and offers than the next guy. Test more bids and have different camps running at different bids targeting placements that are profitable at each bid.

    Spy to make sure you're running the best offer you could be running in a certain geo, that you have access to. Have access to more good-quality traffic so you can scale faster and bigger than the next guy (this is where the investment mentioned above comes in).

    Build a stellar relationship with your affiliate managers. Meet them in person (conferences etc.) Take them out to dinner. Send them gifts. And above all, show them you can do volume - even if you need to run at a slight loss; just to get to that pay bump, and/or to set yourself apart from the newbies.

    So that next time there's a good offer, they'll think of leaving you some cap.

    Also: Once you've demonstrated your abilities, and have built up some cash flow, you can approach direct advertisers. They don't pay as often (thus the need for cashflow), but payouts are typically better.

    And this is where you can negotiate custom-made offers that nobody but you would be running.

    If all this sounds like a lot of work - it is. And if you're not excited and passionate about running pop, I would suggest to explore other traffic types.

    Like push, or native, or facebook, or adwords, or seo. Even email.

    Also remember that affiliate offers aren't the only option. There's the selling of physical goods (ecom), buying and selling leads (lead gen), running paid traffic for people that own a product/service (media buying / agency), or promote your own product/service (like how I'm setting up my own energy healing practice).

    Now that you have a bit of experience with paid traffic, it may be a good time to really think about your options and what you WANT to be doing in the long run, and go from there.

    Doing something you enjoy makes all the challenges much easier to face, and life easier to live.

    Just my 2 cents.

    AM is a business, so you must treat it as a business.

    Choosing the right offer is the key to a successful campaign, so the offer page itself must be good. I compare it to food, must look good, must smell good and must taste good.

    Next is choosing the proper traffic source. Not all traffic source will cover the needed targeting that can make a cost effective campaign, except search traffic.

    Because you need to target your audience by (age, gender and ethnicity relative to the product) search traffic like FB, YT and IG are the best but costliest due to competition. MA and GA are still affordable but not as good, much better than the mainstream traffic. When you are modestly successful and you budget is bigger than try the latter.
